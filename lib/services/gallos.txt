I. General Management and Control Principles
Effective gamecock management requires a lifelong commitment and full-time attention. It's an arduous but potentially rewarding endeavor. The primary goal is continuous improvement year after year.
Key Principles:
• Knowledge Acquisition: Continuously gather information about gamefowl breeding by visiting experienced breeders, reading materials, watching videos, and engaging with online communities.
• Budget and Time Management: Plan all activities within your financial and time limits. A large farm is not a prerequisite for quality gamefowl.
• Selection and Culling: Regularly evaluate birds and be willing to discard defective or inferior individuals to maintain quality and prevent unnecessary costs.
• Respect for Animals: Treat the birds with respect, prioritizing their well-being above all else.
• Professionalism: Recognize that the industry involves various professionals (vets, nutritionists, organizers, judges) and adhere to established practices.
Data to Register for Overall Control:
• Farm/Breeder Information: Name, location, contact.
• Goals/Objectives: Annual improvement targets (e.g., target win rates, fertility rates).
• Inventory: Total number of birds (by age, sex, breed).
• Financial Records: Expenses (feed, meds, equipment, labor), income (sales, winnings).
II. Breeding (Crianza)
Breeding is the foundation for producing high-quality gamecocks and is where true galleros are made. It's about giving life to impressive birds and constantly improving them.
A. Selection of Broodstock (Reproductores)
• Prioritize Proven Bloodlines: Start with proven bloodlines from established, winning breeders to avoid expensive mistakes.
• Lineage Research: Understand the family tree (pedigree) of desired bloodlines.
• Genetic Purity: Focus on working with homozygous animals (as close to pure as possible) to ensure consistent transmission of desired characteristics across generations. Avoid random mixing of lines.
• Ideal Characteristics for Reproducers:
    ◦ Fineza (Bravery/Valor): Incessant determination to kill the opponent.
    ◦ Prepotencia (Transmissibility): Ability to transmit the best characteristics to offspring.
    ◦ Salud (Health): Vigor, robustness, good appetite, and consistent aggressiveness.
    ◦ Fuerza (Strength): Necessary to use weapons effectively and inflict damage.
    ◦ Habilidad de Cortar (Cutting Ability): Crucial for winning fights.
    ◦ Physical Traits: Round body, good musculature, adequate stature (not short), lively red eyes, ample and abundant feathers.
    ◦ Fighting Style: Fast, cutting, very aggressive; constantly attacking the opponent without seeking merely to bite.
• Selecting Hens: Often overlooked but crucial. Hens should have good blood and extraordinary qualities, sometimes being more expensive than roosters. Look for: well-cared plumage, ample and well-defined breast, bright red crest and wattles, triangular head with short beak and little feathering behind the crest, prominent and bright orange eyes, wide abdomen, moist cloaca, and arrogance (character).
• Selecting Roosters: Ideally, select roosters you have personally seen fight. Look for: good size (not fat), good plumage, prominent orange eyes, bright red face, adequate leg-body ratio, ample breast, slightly bent elbows, high natural posture, and a wide back.
• Age of Broodstock: Proven hens should be at least two years old. Young brood cocks can be used, or vice-versa.
B. Mating Methods
• Timing: Most breeders perform crossings from January to April to ensure chicks are large enough to withstand the rainy season's humidity and cold.
• Individual Crosses: Recommended for better control and tracking which pairs produce the best results.
• Group Breeding: One cock with a group of hens.
• Specific Breeding Methods:
    ◦ In-breeding: Mating closely related birds (brother/sister, father/daughter). Advantages: Concentrates desired traits and eliminates undesired ones quickly over 3-4 generations. Disadvantages: High risk of genetic defects, lower fertility, increased disease susceptibility, skeletal issues. Requires extensive genetic knowledge.
    ◦ Line-breeding: Crossing birds from the same "line" (e.g., father-granddaughter, half-siblings, cousins). Most common for serious breeders as it incorporates virtues of both parents without doubling the risks of severe defects seen in in-breeding.
    ◦ Out-crossing: Introducing new, unrelated blood to a strain.
    ◦ Cross-breeding: Mating different breeds to combine specific qualities (e.g., power-speed blend like Ruble Hatch and Black Traveler). Not for serious breeders for general purposes.
    ◦ Three-way/Four-way Crosses: More complex crosses involving three or four different strains to combine specific characteristics.
C. Egg Collection and Incubation
• Collection: Collect eggs 2-3 times a day to maintain control and identification.
• Handling: Handle eggs with clean hands, do not agitate or strike them.
• Selection: Eggs should be uniform in size (neither too small nor too large). Reject dirty or cracked eggs.
• Marking: Mark eggs with pencil (date, lot, or hen number) on the wider, air chamber side.
• Storage: Store in a cool place (10-18°C), with the wide end up, ideally on a bed of fresh earth in hot climates. Do not let more than 7 days pass between laying and incubation.
• Incubation Parameters (21 days):
    ◦ Days 1-17: 37.7-38°C with 55% humidity.
    ◦ Days 18-21: 37°C with up to 80% humidity (to prevent chicks sticking to shell).
• Fertility Check: Can be done by illuminating with a light source, checking for solid shell, firm yolk, small air chamber, and slow yolk movement.
• Incubation Success Rate: A good rate is 79-80%, with some breeders claiming 90-95%.
D. Chick Rearing
• Brooding: Use brooders with heating elements. For the first week, place newspaper over grates to prevent leg injuries.
• Feeding: For the first 2 days, spread food on newspaper so chicks learn to eat. Then use low feeders. Clean feeders daily to prevent fungi.
• Initial Diet: "Iniciarina, Bioestartina" or Purina pap. If bloody droppings occur, switch to "Iniciarina Premium," add refined corn and fresh alfalfa, and administer Sulfa-K or Delsulf in water.
• Supplements (Optional): Chopped vegetables (Chinese onion, tomato, alfalfa, carrot) twice a week. Cooked egg with boiled quinoa or oatmeal, a little sugar, and breadcrumbs. PVM and Protiban are good nutritional complements.
• Space: As they grow, move chicks to larger spaces, eventually to outdoor "voladeros" (flying cages) at 6-7 months.
• Sunlight: Expose chicks to sun for at least 4 hours daily for Vitamin D.
• Predator Protection: Ensure coops are secure at night against skunks, possums, and other predators. Pyrenees Giant dogs are known to protect gamecock farms.
E. Genetic Data to Register:
• Pedigree: Detailed family tree (parents, grandparents, etc.) for each bird.
• Line/Strain: Identification of the specific genetic line or strain.
• Breeding Method Used: In-breeding, Line-breeding, Out-crossing, Cross-breeding, etc..
• Hatch Dates: Date of birth for each chick.
• Egg Records: Number of eggs laid per hen, fertility percentage, hatching percentage.
• Chick Records: Number of chicks hatched, number raised, sex ratio (males/females).
• Physical Characteristics of Offspring: Color, feathering, comb type, leg color, and any observed deformities or defects.
• Performance of Offspring: Notes on combativeness, health, growth rate, and overall quality as they mature.
III. Training (Entrenamiento)
Training is a combination of physical conditioning, skill development, and mental fortitude. The training method must match the rooster's structure and fighting style.
A. Health and Nutrition During Training
• Dietary Balance: Provide a balanced diet rich in high-quality protein, carbohydrates, vitamins, and minerals. Grains (corn, wheat, peas, oats, lentils, sorghum) and specialized feeds are common.
• Feeding Frequency: Many small portions throughout the day are recommended to stabilize blood sugar and maintain high energy levels.
• Supplementation:
    ◦ General Purpose: Multivitamins and minerals for overall well-being.
    ◦ Muscle Building: Amino acids and protein supplements for anabolism, especially for "fast-twitch" muscles crucial for quick, sprint-like fights.
    ◦ Blood Conditioning & Oxygenation: B complex, B12, and iron to improve oxygen transport. Vasodilators like B15 help blood flow and heat dissipation.
    ◦ Energy Generation: Carbohydrates before workouts (e.g., 10% grains/cracked corn). Creatine, ribose, ATP, glucose, and other sugars are crucial for anaerobic energy pathways.
    ◦ Hormones/Steroids (Performance Enhancing Drugs): While some use them (e.g., testosterone, epinefrina, adrenalina, caffeine, tiroxina), it involves risks due to a rooster's sensitive nature, and can lead to over-aggressiveness or artificial performance. Natural anabolic phytosterols are an alternative.
    ◦ Hydration: Crucial to maintain proper body moisture. Focus on intracellular hydration to promote recovery and prevent issues from dehydration (cardiovascular strain, heat stress) or over-hydration (sluggishness, reduced cutting ability, hemolysis). Alkaline agents (egg white, alkaline water, probiotics, fruits) can delay fatigue by counteracting lactic acid buildup.
• Health Checks & Prevention: Regular health checks are vital. Maintain a preventive healthcare program including vaccinations (Marek at day 1, Newcastle, Fowl Pox, Coryza) and parasite control (delousing every 2 weeks, especially hens before breeding). Treat colds with antibiotics.
B. Physical Conditioning & Skill Development
• Starting Age: Begin training when roosters are around 10-11 months old, or when they show willingness to fight. Optimal fighting age is 2.5 to 3 years.
• Consistency and Rest: Train at least 3-4 times per week, ensuring adequate rest periods to prevent overtraining, injuries, and reduced performance. A general rule suggests working roosters once a week to allow for muscle recovery.
• Environment: Ensure spacious areas for exercise and exposure to varied terrains/weather.
• Basic Exercises:
    ◦ Voladas (Flying bursts): For agility and speed, using obstacles to improve reflexes.
    ◦ Verticales y Laterales (Vertical & Lateral Jumps): For explosive leg power, muscle strengthening, balance, and coordination.
    ◦ Ejercicio del 8 (Figure-Eight Exercise): To improve coordination, reflexes, and balance by following an '8' pattern.
• Advanced Techniques:
    ◦ Pajeo: Controlled exercises with short, intense bursts and brief rests to build cardiovascular endurance.
    ◦ Correr en la Jaula (Cage Running): Confined space running to build resistance, cardiovascular system, lung capacity, and maneuverability.
    ◦ Entrenamiento con Cuerda (Rope Training): Climbing, swinging, pulling for overall strength and flexibility.
• Sparring (Tope): Essential for developing fighting instincts and adapting to ring actions.
    ◦ Use padded apparatuses or trained sparring partners.
    ◦ Gradually increase intensity and duration. For practice, rooster's spurs should be covered, and training rooster's beak covered.
    ◦ Use different sparring partners (topetones, monas, piedras) with varied styles (runners, high fighters, inside fighters) to prepare for diverse opponents.
• Cronogram (Example 13-Week Schedule) - Adapted from Salinas:
    ◦ Weeks 1: 7 mins (4 min forced run/figure-eights, 2 min covered-spur sparring, 1 min moderate flying).
    ◦ Weeks 2-3: 9 mins (first 7 as Week 1, last 2 min open-beak sparring with similar rooster).
    ◦ Weeks 4-5: 12 mins (3 min sparring with 2oz heavier, covered beak; 7 min as Week 1; 2 min sparring with 2oz lighter/faster, covered beak).
    ◦ Weeks 6-10: 15 mins (5 min sparring with 2oz heavier, covered beak; 7 min as Week 1; 3 min sparring with 2oz lighter/faster, covered beak). Alternate open-beak sparring every other week.
    ◦ Week 11: 12 mins (same as Weeks 4-5).
    ◦ Week 12: 7 min run, then 1 hour with a hen (for psychological well-being).
    ◦ Week 13: Complete rest before combat.
• Pre-Fight Rituals: Rotate roosters in small circles to prevent dizziness. Wash with soap and water, then alcohol. Observe the rooster's mood and routine; changes in sleeping position can indicate poor rest.
C. Grooming (Alistamiento)
• Descreste (Dubbing/Comb Removal): Removing the comb and wattles.
    ◦ Timing: Can be done from chick stage, but preferable after testing, or when roosters are 1 to 1.5 years old. Avoid during hot weather. Best done in the afternoon and during a waning moon to reduce bleeding.
    ◦ Procedure: No anesthesia. Secure the rooster. Use sharp scissors. Cut ears, then wattles, then the comb (almost level with the nape, with a small protuberance near the beak for protection). The goal is to prevent the opponent from holding onto these areas and to avoid bleeding into the eyes.
• Tusar (Trimming/Clipping): Cutting plumage in specific areas (sides, thighs, under breast/wings, belly to tail, knees, back, neck) for better performance and appearance. Use barber scissors, lemon juice (to adhere feathers), and a sponge. Clean the rooster with soap and water after trimming.
D. Data to Register for Training:
• Training Schedule: Dates and duration of each exercise session.
• Exercise Type: Details of specific exercises performed (e.g., voladas, sparring).
• Performance Metrics: Observations on stamina, speed, reflexes, strength during training.
• Sparring Partners: Type and characteristics of roosters used for sparring.
• Weight Tracking: Regular weight measurements during conditioning.
• Diet & Supplements: Detailed records of feed type, quantities, and supplements given.
• Health Observations: Any signs of injury, illness, or stress during training.
• Grooming Records: Dates of dubbing and trimming.
IV. Combat (Combate)
Cockfighting involves strict rules and preparations to ensure a "fair" contest.
A. Preparation for Combat
• Weighing & Registration: Roosters must be presented in good condition, clean, and weigh within the established limits (e.g., 1.900 Kg to 2.680 Kg in Mexico). A slight deviation (up to 100g over) may be acceptable if the rooster is armed on the right leg.
• Matching: Fights are matched by weight equality. Computer systems are used in derbies for precise matching.
• Arming the Gallo Navajero: This is a crucial skill.
    ◦ Tools: Calza (holder), adjustment fabric, blade support, thread, blade, cover, spur saw, protectors (for sparring), scissors.
    ◦ Blade Type: In Mexico, typically a steel blade of 1 inch (or 1 inch 2 lines max) that forms a 90° angle from tip to base. Can have one or two edges. The "Hoja de Olivo" is a traditional Peruvian blade.
    ◦ Procedure: Trim the spur flat to the support. Place adjustment fabric and blade support firmly. Use thread to tightly secure the blade, making crosses and simple turns. The blade should be at a right angle to the spur. Finish with secure knots. Cover the blade until the fight.
• Antidoping: Crucial for honest fights. Roosters should be clean of grease, medications, or other substances.
    ◦ Checks: Before the fight, clean the blades/spurs with lemon. For spurs, sand lightly and use a magnet to check for hidden iron.
    ◦ Detection Methods: Solutions (distilled water, alcohol, carbon tetrachloride, acetone) are used with a reactive acid (lead acetate) to detect substances. Positive results show cloudiness or residues.
    ◦ Drugs: Avoid using performance-enhancing drugs as they can mask natural ability and have side effects.
B. During the Fight
• Duration: Maximum fight duration varies (e.g., 15-20 minutes for commitments, 10-15 minutes for derbies).
• Officials: The arena judge is the supreme authority, whose verdicts are final and unappealable. Handlers are responsible for the rooster's management during the fight.
• Handler Conduct: Must be visible, wear assigned cockade, and provide all legitimate aid to help their rooster win. Cannot advance or chase the rooster until the first blow is exchanged.
• Mandatory Blade Change: In some rules, a mandatory blade change occurs 3 minutes into the fight.
• Prohibitions: Strictly forbidden to remove roosters from the ring (unless for an alternate fight zone), or for anyone other than the judge and handlers to intervene.
• Defining the Winner/Loser:
    ◦ Gallo Muerto (Dead Rooster): A rooster that is stiff, makes uncoordinated movements, or shows no signs of life.
    ◦ Fondo (Quit/Yield): A rooster that lies down with its beak fixed to the ground for a ten-second count.
    ◦ Huido (Fleeing): A rooster that abandons combat twice consecutively.
    ◦ Rebeldía (Rebellion): A handler's refusal to present their rooster for inspection or fight.
    ◦ A dead or "fondeado" rooster wins against a "huido" one.
    ◦ A rooster that doesn't fight for one minute loses.
    ◦ Broken leg/foot leads to immediate loss. Broken wing (near body) leads to 50% loss.
    ◦ If both roosters stop fighting, it's a draw after one minute.
    ◦ A rooster constantly picking, even while down, does not incur a "losing" count.
• Fight Phases: Can be divided into three thirds:
    ◦ First Third: Definitive, with good aim and violence.
    ◦ Second Third: Equality predominates.
    ◦ Third Third: Reveals the true casta (bravery) of the birds, aided by their physical structure and preparation. Luck can also play a role.
C. Post-Combat
• Winning Roosters: Some clean winners may be returned for future fights (after a year of rest and feather change, max 2-3 times). Others, especially those with severe injuries, might be given away to new enthusiasts (to foster the hobby) or disposed of.
• Curative Care: Injured roosters, even winners, often require extensive care and stitching ("cosida" or "curada").
D. Data to Register for Combat:
• Fight Records: Unique ID for each fight.
• Rooster ID: Which rooster participated.
• Weight: Pre-fight weight.
• Date and Location: Date and venue of the fight.
• Opponent Details: Opponent's rooster ID (if available), weight.
• Weapon Used: Type of blade/spur.
• Outcome: Win, Loss, Draw (Tabla).
• Fight Type: Commitment, Derby, Exhibition.
• Fight Notes: Injuries sustained, handler's performance, judge's decisions, noteworthy moments (e.g., "fondo," "huido").
• Post-Fight Status: Recovery progress, if used for breeding later.

General Health and Care Principles
• Preventive processes are crucial and more cost-effective than treating sick animals.
• Strict hygiene and cleanliness are fundamental for preventing diseases in coops, feeders, and waterers.
• Adequate housing conditions are vital, including sufficient space, good ventilation, comfortable perches, proper drainage, protection from elements (rain, cold, excessive wind), and freedom from insects and rodents.
• Balanced nutrition is paramount from the chicks' first days, as it affects their entire life. Roosters require a specific diet based on their development stage and activity level (e.g., growth, training, molting, reproduction).
• Water is the most important nutrient; roosters can live without food longer than without water. Proper hydration is key for performance, with a focus on intracellular hydration to avoid both dehydration and excessive body moisture, which can negatively impact performance.
• Regular health checks are necessary to monitor well-being and detect signs of illness or injury, such as lethargy, abnormal behavior, or physical wounds.
Common Diseases and Health Issues
The sources detail various diseases affecting fighting cocks, including their characteristic signs, means of transmission, prevention, and treatment:
• Ascaridiosis: Caused by a parasite, leading to anemia, low egg production, weakness, and intestinal obstruction. Transmitted orally. Prevention involves cleanliness and dry environments.
• Bronchitis Infecciosa (Infectious Bronchitis): A viral disease causing difficult breathing, reduced egg production (with shell abnormalities), conjunctivitis, sneezing, coughing, and white diarrhea. Transmitted through direct contact. Prevention includes cleanliness, new bedding, and vaccination.
• Coccidiosis: Caused by parasites, resulting in significant weight loss, lethargy, diarrhea, and paralysis. Transmitted orally through contaminated water and food. Specific treatments are available.
• Corisa Infecciosa (Infectious Coryza): A bacterial infection with symptoms like tearing, conjunctivitis, facial swelling, fever, sneezing, coughing, and foul-smelling nasal mucus. Transmitted orally and by air. Prevention includes cleaning, ventilation control, and quarantining new animals.
• Cólera Aviar (Fowl Cholera): A bacterial disease causing weakness, green-yellow diarrhea, fever, and cyanotic (bluish-black) comb/wattles. Transmitted orally, respiratorily, and by direct contact. Prevention focuses on ventilation, hygiene, and quarantine.
• Colibacilosis: Caused by E. Coli bacteria, leading to dehydration, fever, weight loss, and white-yellow diarrhea. Transmitted orally via contaminated food and water. Cleanliness is key for prevention.
• Encefalomielitis Aviar (Avian Encephalomyelitis): A viral disease resulting in ataxia, nervousness, tremors, and reduced egg production. Transmitted through eggs. Prevention includes vaccination and hygiene.
• Laringo Traquitis Infecciosa (Infectious Laryngotracheitis): A viral infection that causes decreased egg production, eye discharge, difficulty breathing, and bloody droppings. Transmitted by air and secretions. Vaccination and clean bedding are preventive measures.
• Leucosis Aviar (Avian Leukosis): A viral disease causing anemia, weight loss, pale comb, and green diarrhea. Transmitted orally and respiratorily. Prevention includes general cleanliness and eliminating carrier breeders.
• Marek's Disease: A viral disease leading to anorexia, paralysis of wings and limbs, stunted growth, and deformed pupils. Transmitted by air and orally. Vaccination (within 24 hours of birth) and cleanliness are vital.
• Micoplasmosis (Mycoplasmosis): A bacterial disease causing eye foam, facial inflammation, and reduced egg production. Transmitted by air. Prevention involves avoiding cold and stress, and using preventative antibiotics.
• Newcastle Disease: A severe viral infection causing anorexia, neurological signs (tremors, paralysis, twisted neck), respiratory issues, and green diarrhea. Highly contagious via air and orally. Prevention involves strict quarantine, hygiene, and vaccination.
• Salmonellosis or Typhoid: Bacterial infections causing white diarrhea in chicks and green diarrhea in adults. Transmitted orally. Prevention includes quarantine, cleanliness, and vaccination.
• Tiña (Ringworm): A fungal infection presenting as dandruff-like scales on the comb, face, and wattles. Transmitted directly or by air. Treatment involves topical antifungal ointments.
• Tuberculosis: A bacterial disease leading to anorexia, weight loss, fever, and green/yellow diarrhea. Prevention focuses on cleanliness, culling sick animals, and proper ventilation.
• Viruela Aviar (Fowl Pox): A viral disease characterized by skin lesions and scabs, particularly on the face, comb, and wattles. Transmitted through abrasions, wounds, and mosquito bites. Prevention is primarily through vaccination.
Parasites and Injuries
• External parasites (mites and lice) cause discomfort and reduced production; they can be detected by examining feathers and treated with acaricides and fumigation.
• Internal parasites can obstruct and irritate the intestines.
• Physical injuries are common in cockfighting, including lacerations, puncture wounds, broken bones, eye loss, and mutilations, often leading to severe infections (like tetanus or septicemia) or death.
Management and Veterinary Care
• Vaccination programs are essential and vary by location and farm characteristics. Vaccines should be stored and administered correctly, often orally or by injection.
• Antibiotics (like Amoxil, Velamox, Tilogén, Taydel, Ciclosona, Canyon) and other medications are used for specific health issues and as preventative measures.
• Supplementation is common to address nutritional deficiencies, improve performance, and support recovery. This includes vitamins (A, B complex, D, E, K), minerals (calcium, phosphorus, iron), amino acids, and other compounds (creatine, ribose, antioxidants).
• Some trainers use performance-enhancing drugs (like testosterone, epinephrine, caffeine citrate, thyroxine, anabolic steroids), but these are associated with risks and side effects such as over-aggressiveness or general health issues. Natural alternatives like phytosterols (e.g., gamma oryzanol) are mentioned as providing similar benefits without the negative side effects.
• Descreste (dubbing) and desbarbado (wattling), the removal of the comb and wattles, are common practices in fighting cocks. This is often done to prevent injuries during fights and can be performed without anesthesia, although some care is taken to minimize bleeding and infection. The comb also plays a role in temperature regulation and health indication.
• Consulting a local veterinarian is recommended for disease diagnosis and vaccination planning.

Salud y Enfermedades Comunes en Gallos
Las fuentes proporcionan información detallada sobre la salud de los gallos, las enfermedades comunes, así como prácticas de prevención, tratamiento y cuidado general. La prevención es un pilar fundamental en la salud de los gallos de pelea, siendo más económica que el tratamiento de animales enfermos.
Principios Generales de Salud y Cuidado
• Higiene y Limpieza: Esencial para prevenir enfermedades en gallineros, comederos y bebederos. La limpieza diaria de los restos de alimento es crucial para evitar hongos y fermentación. Los bebederos y comederos deben estar ligeramente elevados y lejos del sol para evitar contaminación con heces o tierra.
• Condiciones de Alojamiento Adecuadas: Incluyen suficiente espacio, buena ventilación, perchas cómodas, drenaje adecuado del piso, protección contra elementos (lluvia, frío, viento excesivo), y estar libres de insectos y roedores. La mezcla de tierra o viruta en el piso ayuda a mantener el calor y absorber la humedad, previniendo enfermedades. No se deben criar más aves de las que se puedan atender.
• Nutrición Balanceada: Crucial desde los primeros días de los pollitos, ya que afecta su vida entera. Los gallos de pelea requieren una dieta específica según su etapa (crecimiento, entrenamiento, muda, reproducción).
    ◦ Agua: Es el nutriente más importante; los gallos pueden vivir más tiempo sin comida que sin agua. La hidratación adecuada, enfocada en la hidratación intracelular, es clave para el rendimiento y para evitar deshidratación o humedad excesiva. Se recomienda agua purificada en lugar de agua del grifo, especialmente antes de una pelea, para un ambiente alcalino.
    ◦ Componentes Dietéticos:
        ▪ Grasas e Hidratos de Carbono: Fuentes de energía. El exceso de grasa puede acumularse en el cuerpo.
        ▪ Vitaminas: Sustancias naturales esenciales para el metabolismo. La luz solar es una fuente importante de vitamina D. Alimentos como hígado de bacalao, levadura de cerveza, alfalfa y yema de huevo son ricos en vitaminas. La deficiencia de vitaminas puede llevar a problemas de visión, sistema nervioso, anemia, parálisis y retraso en el crecimiento.
        ▪ Minerales: Calcio, fósforo, yodo, cobre, silicio (avena); hierro, fosfato de cal (cebada); calcio (maíz); potasio, sodio, magnesio, hierro, flúor (mijo). El "Grit" (piedrecitas) es indispensable para la digestión.
        ▪ Proteínas y Aminoácidos: Esenciales para el desarrollo y reparación muscular. Fuentes incluyen torta de soya, harina de carne/pescado, levadura de cerveza, sardinas, corazón e hígado de res.
• Chequeos de Salud Regulares: Necesarios para monitorear el bienestar y detectar signos de enfermedad o lesión.
Enfermedades Comunes y Problemas de Salud
Las fuentes detallan varias enfermedades que afectan a los gallos de pelea, incluyendo sus signos, transmisión, prevención y tratamiento:
Enfermedades Bacterianas
• Corisa Infecciosa (Infectious Coryza):
    ◦ Signos: Lagrimeo, conjuntivitis, hinchazón facial, fiebre, estornudos, tos, mucosidad nasal maloliente y espumosa en el ojo.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía oral y respiratoria, por aire contaminado o contacto directo con animales enfermos.
    ◦ Prevención: Limpieza de recipientes, control de ventilación, cuarentena de animales nuevos, separación de enfermos.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Omnipen, A-tresul, Sulfour (solubles). También se usa vacunación.
• Cólera Aviar (Fowl Cholera):
    ◦ Signos: Debilidad, diarrea verde-amarillenta, fiebre, cresta y barbillas cianóticas (azul-negro).
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía oral, respiratoria, y contacto directo.
    ◦ Prevención: Ventilación, higiene, cuarentena, sacrificio de enfermos.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Omnipen, A-tresul, Sulfour (solubles).
• Colibacilosis:
    ◦ Signos: Deshidratación, fiebre, pérdida de peso, artritis, diarrea blanca-amarillenta con moco.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía oral, alimento y agua contaminados.
    ◦ Prevención: Limpieza y desinfección de corrales y recipientes.
    ◦ Tratamiento: NF-180 (tabletas/solubles), NFZ (soluble), Neoterramix 20-20 (polvo).
• Micoplasmosis (Mycoplasmosis):
    ◦ Signos: Espuma en los ojos, inflamación facial, disminución de producción de huevos, baja fertilidad, crecimiento lento, exudado nasal, obstrucción de la tráquea.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía aérea, por contacto con portadores (necesita un agente desencadenante).
    ◦ Prevención: Evitar ambientes fríos, estrés, cambios bruscos de alimentación, uso de antibióticos preventivos.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Ericlor (inyección), Tylan (soluble), Cloramidina (inyección), Tiamutin (soluble).
• Salmonelosis o Tifoidea:
    ◦ Signos: Diarrea blanca en pollitos, diarrea verde en adultos.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía oral, alimentos y agua contaminados.
    ◦ Prevención: Cuarentena, limpieza, vacunación.
    ◦ Tratamiento: NF-180 (pastillas), Novamixin (polvo).
• Tuberculosis:
    ◦ Signos: Anorexia, pérdida de peso, fiebre, diarrea verde o amarillenta, resequedad de plumas.
    ◦ Prevención: Limpieza y desinfección general, sacrificio de animales enfermos, separación por edades, buena ventilación, evitar humedad.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Consultar a un veterinario zootecnista.
Enfermedades Virales
• Bronquitis Infecciosa (Infectious Bronchitis):
    ◦ Signos: Dificultad para respirar, reducción en la producción de huevos (con cáscaras anormales), conjuntivitis, estornudos, tos, diarrea blanca.
    ◦ Transmisión: Contacto directo.
    ◦ Prevención: Limpieza, cambio de cama, vacunación. Puede ser activada por acidez o alcalinidad del agua con minerales.
• Encefalomielitis Aviar (Avian Encephalomyelitis):
    ◦ Signos: Ataxia, nerviosismo, temblores de cuello y cabeza, reducción en la producción de huevos.
    ◦ Transmisión: A través del huevo, los pollitos nacen infectados.
    ◦ Prevención: Vacunación, cuarentena, limpieza de huevos y nidos.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Vacuna contra Encefalomielitis.
• Laringotraqueítis Infecciosa (Infectious Laryngotracheitis):
    ◦ Signos: Disminución de producción de huevos, secreción ocular, dificultad respiratoria, estertores, pico caído, secreción nasal, tos, sinusitis, excremento con espuma y algo de sangre.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía aérea y por secreciones. Los animales sanos pueden ser portadores.
    ◦ Prevención: Limpieza general, cuarentena de animales nuevos, eliminación de reproductores portadores.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Consultar a un veterinario zootecnista.
• Leucosis Aviar (Avian Leukosis):
    ◦ Signos: Anemia, pérdida de peso, cresta pálida, diarrea verde.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía oral y respiratoria.
    ◦ Prevención: Limpieza general, eliminación de reproductores portadores.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Sacrificio.
• Enfermedad de Marek (Marek's Disease):
    ◦ Signos: Anorexia, caída del pecho, apoyo en el suelo, respiración con pico abierto, cresta pálida y escamosa (cianótica), disminución de producción de huevos, retraso del crecimiento, pupila unilateral deforme, dedos enrollados, cuello estirado hacia adelante/atrás, diarrea acuosa, asfixia, parálisis de alas y miembros.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía aérea y oral, germen en el aire contamina alimento y agua.
    ◦ Prevención: Vacunación (en el primer día de vida, antes de las 24 horas), limpieza y desinfección.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Sacrificio.
• Enfermedad de Newcastle (Newcastle Disease):
    ◦ Signos: Anorexia, disminución de producción de huevos (con cáscaras rugosas y deformes), diarrea verde, estornudos, temblor corporal y de cabeza, parálisis, plumas erizadas, postración, fiebre, decaimiento, apatía, pérdida de coordinación, tos, secreción nasal, cuello torcido con cabeza hacia atrás, pico abierto, dedos enroscados, ojos cerrados, camina de lado y hacia atrás.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía aérea y oral, aire contaminado, animales nuevos infectados, visitas de personas de otros criaderos.
    ◦ Prevención: Cuarentena estricta, higiene, vacunación (rutinaria cada 4 meses para evitar que las defensas bajen).
• Viruela Aviar (Fowl Pox):
    ◦ Signos: Granos en la piel (cara, cresta, barbilla), costras en la piel, membranas blancas o amarillas dentro del pico, garganta, lengua y párpados.
    ◦ Transmisión: Excoriaciones, heridas, piquetes de mosquitos.
    ◦ Prevención: Vacunación (tipo pollo, tipo paloma), limpieza y desinfección de animales que han topado (cara, pico, patas), uso controlado de antibióticos para prevenir infecciones.
    ◦ Tratamiento: Vacuna contra Viruela. Limpiar las lesiones con desinfectante y aplicar azul de metileno o violeta de Genciana.
Parásitos
• Ascaridiosis:
    ◦ Causa: Parásito (Ascaridia Galli).
    ◦ Signos: Anemia, baja producción de huevos, debilidad, caquexia (desnutrición), melena con moco, obstrucción intestinal.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía oral (alimentos o agua contaminada).
    ◦ Prevención: Limpieza y ambientes secos.
• Coccidiosis:
    ◦ Causa: Parásitos (Eimerias).
    ◦ Signos: Pérdida considerable de peso, decaimiento, pesadez, diarrea, parálisis, plumas erizadas, retraso del crecimiento.
    ◦ Transmisión: Vía oral (agua y alimento contaminados con excremento).
    ◦ Tratamiento: NF-180 (pastillas), NFZ (soluble), Coccistat-45 (polvo), Sufaquinoxilina (soluble).
    ◦ Prevención: Cambiar papel periódico diariamente en criadoras para prevenir coccidiosis y diarreas en pollitos.
• Parásitos Externos (Ectoparásitos): Ácaros y piojos.
    ◦ Síntomas: Baja postura, plumas frágiles, molestias y nerviosismo. Se detectan por examen minucioso.
    ◦ Prevención: Desinfección de equipos e instalaciones. Pueden ser erradicados bañando las aves con acaricidas (como CEBÍN EN POLVO SOLUBLE) y fumigando corrales.
Lesiones y Otros Problemas
• Lesiones Físicas: Comunes en peleas, incluyen laceraciones, heridas punzantes, fracturas, pérdida de ojos y mutilaciones. Estas pueden llevar a infecciones graves (tétanos, septicemia) o la muerte. Es un riesgo inherente a la actividad.
• Cresta y Barbillas: La cresta es un indicador de salud y ayuda a regular la temperatura. Su alteración (resequedad, color azulado) puede ser señal de enfermedad. El descreste (corte de cresta) y desbarbado (corte de barbillas) son prácticas comunes para evitar lesiones en combate, aunque pueden causar anemia, estrés e infecciones.
• Comer tierra: Puede ser un hábito en gallos.
Manejo y Atención Veterinaria
• Programas de Vacunación: Esenciales y varían según la zona y criadero. Deben ser planificados con un médico veterinario. Las vacunas deben almacenarse a baja temperatura y usarse inmediatamente tras abrir.
    ◦ Vías de Administración: Oral, intramuscular, subcutánea, intradérmica, ocular/nasal, por aspersión.
• Medicamentos y Suplementos:
    ◦ Antibióticos: Para diversas infecciones y como preventivo. Es más efectivo administrarlos mezclados con alimento mojado que solo en el agua.
    ◦ Vitaminas y Minerales: Suplementos para el bienestar general, anabolismo, acondicionamiento sanguíneo, circulación de oxígeno y generación de energía. Se mencionan complejos B, B12, hierro, fósforo, magnesio.
    ◦ Suplementos para Rendimiento: Creatina y ribosa para energía. Gamma Oryzanol (fitosterol natural) proporciona beneficios similares a los esteroides anabólicos sin efectos secundarios como la agresividad excesiva, mejorando los niveles hormonales y la fuerza.
    ◦ Drogas Estimulantes: Se advierte contra el uso de drogas para mejorar el rendimiento, como la nuez vómica (estricnina) y la tiroxina, debido a riesgos y la alteración del valor natural del gallo.
    ◦ Detección de Sustancias: Métodos para detectar el uso de drogas en gallos antes de una pelea, utilizando soluciones como agua destilada y alcohol con reactivos.
• Desinfección: Fumigación periódica con productos como Yodine, Dodigen, Formol, Kreso es crucial para prevenir la aparición de bacterias y virus. El cepillado de los gallos con agua y jabón, seguido de alcohol, es parte de la rutina de cuidado.
• Veterinario: La consulta con un médico veterinario zootecnista local es recomendada para el diagnóstico de enfermedades y la planificación de vacunaciones. Los laboratorios también realizan pruebas de enfermedades
